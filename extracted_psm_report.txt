CHAPTER 1:  INTRODUCTION 
 
The current landscape of road safety technology is heavily biased toward larger 
motor vehicles, often leaving bicycle and electric scooter riders exposed to severe 
infrastructural hazards. As micro -mobility solutions become increasingly integrated 
into urb an transit networks, the vulnerability of these riders has become a critical 
public safety concern. A primary threat to micro -mobility users is surface -induced 
accidents caused by road defects such as potholes. Due to their small wheel diameters 
and freque ntly rigid suspension systems, a minor pothole that a car might easily 
traverse can cause an e -scooter rider to suffer an immediate loss of control or 
mechanical failure. 
While standard GPS applications provide effective route guidance, they lack the 
real-time granular data necessary to warn users about immediate, localized changes in 
road surface quality. Furthermore, existing safety tools, such as standard dashcams, 
are p redominantly reactive . they record accidents post -occurrence rather than 
preventing them. Consequently, there is an urgent demand for a proactive, real -time 
warning system capable of identifying hazards as they appear and assessing their 
specific threat level based on the rider's current state. 
This study aims to bridge this safety gap by developing a proactive hazard 
detection framework specifically tailored to the micro -mobility community. The 
primary objectives are threefold: (1) to train a lightweight Convolutional Neural 
Network (CNN) capable of identifying potholes in real -time on a mobile device; (2) 
to implement a dynamic Risk Assessment Algorithm that calculates accident 
probability by correlating pothole dimensions with real-time vehicle velocity; and (3) 
to design a non-intrusive mobile interface that delivers immediate safety warnings via 
audio and visual cues. The scope of the proposed system focuses on a fully offline 
Android application utilizing TensorFlow Lite for on-device edge computing, ensuring 
user privacy, zero latency, and independence from network coverage.

CHAPTER 2:  LITERATURE REVIEW AND PROJECT METHODOLOGY 
 
Road hazard detection has historically relied on tactile or vibration-based 
sensors. Research by Kim et al. (2021) explored the use of smartphone 
accelerometers to detect potholes. While effective at mapping road conditions, this 
method is inherently reactive, identifying the hazard only after the impact has 
occurred. For micro-mobility users, vision-based detection is vastly superior as it 
facilitates proactive warnings, allowing riders to avoid hazards entirely before a 
collision. 
 
The advent of Deep Learning, particularly the "You Only Look Once" (YOLO) 
architecture, has revolutionized real-time object detection by treating bounding box 
prediction as a single regression problem. Earlier iterations, such as YOLOv3 and 
YOLOv4, established the feasibility of road damage detection but demanded high-
performance GPUs, rendering them unsuitable for mobile deployment. Recent 
advancements, namely YOLOv8 and YOLOv11 Nano, utilize advanced feature 
extraction and optimized architectures specifically designed for edge devices. These 
models offer a critical balance between Mean Average Precision (mAP) and 
inference speed, which is indispensable for real-time detection on a moving bicycle 
or scooter. 
 
To achieve a "fully offline" system, on-device edge computing is facilitated 
through TensorFlow Lite (TFLite). This framework allows complex deep learning 
models to run directly on a smartphone’s CPU or Neural Processing Unit (NPU). By 
employing weight quantization which is converting 32-bit floating-point parameters 
into 8-bit integers to make the model size is significantly reduced and inference 
latency is improved without substantial degradation in accuracy. By processing data 
locally, user privacy is maintained since no camera feed is uploaded to cloud servers, 
and the system remains robust in areas with poor cellular reception. 
 
Despite these technological advancements, existing literature focuses almost 
exclusively on motor vehicles. Micro-mobility requires a specialized risk assessment 
model because the threat posed by a pothole is inextricably linked to the vehicle's

CHAPTER 3:  REQUIREMENT ANALYSIS 
The proposed methodology follows a structured AI development lifecycle, 
ensuring the model is optimized for the specific computational and physical constraints 
of handlebar-mounted mobile devices. The framework is divided into four primary 
phases: Data Acquisition, Model Optimization, Algorithm Development, and System 
Architecture. 
 
3.1 Data Acquisition and Pre-processing 
The accuracy of the vision-based detector is heavily dependent on the quality 
and diversity of the training data. This project utilizes the Japan Road Damage Dataset, 
sourced via the Roboflow platform, to ensure the model recognizes a wide variety of 
pothole morphologies and asphalt conditions. To simulate real -world micro-mobility 
riding conditions, the dataset undergoes rigorous data augmentation. Images are 
subjected to motion blur to simulate vehicle vibration, brightness variations to account 
for diver se weather and lighting conditions, and rotational adjustments to 
accommodate various smartphone mounting angles on handlebars. 
3.2 Model Training and Edge Optimization 
The model is trained using the PyTorch framework in conjunction with the 
Ultralytics YOLO architecture. Training is executed on a high -performance 
workstation equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 3060 GPU to handle data pre-processing 
and CUDA -accelerated model com pilation. To ensure the model meets the strict 
latency requirements of a mobile environment, a smaller input image size (320x320) 
is utilized, allowing the model to process fewer pixels and massively accelerating 
inference speed. 
Once the desired Mean Average Precision (mAP) is achieved, the PyTorch 
model is exported to the. tflite format. During this export process, INT8 quantization 
is applied to shrink the model size by approximately a factor of four. This optimization

3.4 System Architecture and Application Development 
The deployment application is developed using the Flutter framework, 
ensuring seamless cross-platform integration with the smartphone's camera and GPS 
hardware. The architecture is stratified into three main layers: 
• Input Layer: Captures high -frequency frames from the smartphone 
camera and velocity data from the GPS module. 
• Inference Layer: The TFLite interpreter processes the frames offline 
to detect potholes and extract bounding box coordinates at a target rate 
of at least 15 Frames Per Second (FPS).

• Logic & Alert Layer: The Risk Assessment Algorithm processes the 
AI output and GPS data. When the calculated Risk Index exceeds a 
predefined safety threshold (e.g., 75%), the system triggers immediate, 
non-intrusive warnings. These include a high-frequency audible "beep" 
and a high-contrast visual red overlay on the UI, ensuring the rider is 
alerted under direct sunlight with a total system latency of less than 
200ms.
